Weakly Bounded Bodies

The project studies bodies that remain legible without being fixed, solid, or fully enclosed: semi-corporeal, plasmatic, luminous, and sometimes collective forms that change what embodiment can do.

Core idea

The central claim is that weakly bounded bodies are not just visual novelties. They can function as perceptual, social, and design instruments by softening bodily boundaries while preserving coherence.

The central claim

Semi-corporeal and plasmatic bodies occupy a meaningful middle zone between photorealistic avatars and pure abstraction. They can preserve enough coherence to support bodily readability, interaction, and relation while also opening permeability, multiplicity, and transformation that conventional avatar shells tend to suppress (Wagemans et al.; Johansson; dos Anjos and Pereira; Yee and Bailenson).

In that sense, the project is less about "ethereal aesthetics" than about the design and meaning of weakly bounded bodies: how they stay readable, how they alter self-models, and how they redistribute the line between self, other, and environment (Eisenstein; Whissel; Glowacki).

Four layers hold the project together

Perception

Gestalt grouping and biological-motion research explain why fragmented or sparse bodies can still read as coherent bodies. Bodily legibility does not depend on a sealed surface alone; motion, grouping, and recurrent structure can do the work (Wagemans et al.; Johansson).

Embodiment and interaction

XR research shows that altered self-representation can shape behavior and experience. Unrealistic spectral bodies, abstract or point-cloud avatars, and field-like relational avatars all suggest that embodiment remains available even when the body is no longer a conventional mesh (Richards et al.; dos Anjos and Pereira; Desnoyers-Stewart et al.; Yee and Bailenson).

Genealogy

The historical line runs through plasmaticness, animated spirit, ectoplasmic materialization, and the digital multitude. These traditions supply a vocabulary for mutable form, apparition, collectivity, and transformation rather than treating them as interchangeable atmospherics (Eisenstein; Geil; Wilson; Bukatman; Whissel; Owens).

Translation into systems and spaces

The same body logic can move from avatar form into particles, point clouds, swarm behavior, projection, light, and staged transition. The project treats these not as separate topics but as different material realizations of the same question: how far a body can transform without ceasing to be experienced as a body (Reeves; Reynolds; Liedgren et al.; Glowacki).

What the project is not

It is not a general theory of spirituality, a loose use of quantum language, or a catch-all defense of anything ghostly or atmospheric. Those registers can matter, but only when they sharpen the central problem of weakly bounded embodiment rather than replacing it.

The project stays coherent by returning to the same test: does a concept clarify how transformable bodies maintain legibility while changing relation, perception, or affordance? If not, it belongs elsewhere.

Three reusable claims

  1. Weakly bounded bodies can be more useful than realistic ones when the design goal is transformation rather than resemblance (Richards et al.; dos Anjos and Pereira; Glowacki).
  2. Semi-corporeal embodiment depends less on surface realism than on coherence, motion, and multisensory coupling (Wagemans et al.; Johansson; Desnoyers-Stewart et al.).
  3. Plasmatic and collective body forms are not just visual motifs. They are interface strategies for permeability, relation, and altered self-models (Eisenstein; Whissel; Glowacki et al.; Yee and Bailenson).

The overview essay expands this argument in more detail. This page is meant to keep the project's framing stable as it moves across articles, proposals, and installation work.
