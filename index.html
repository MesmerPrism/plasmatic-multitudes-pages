Semi-Corporeal Avatar Aesthetics

This essay follows a zone between photorealism and abstraction: bodies that can glow, dissolve, swarm, and merge without giving up bodily readability. The question is how such forms stay coherent enough to alter embodiment rather than merely decorate it.

Premise

Weakly bounded bodies are not just visual motifs. Across animation theory, media history, and XR design, they offer a way to soften bodily boundaries while preserving enough coherence for embodiment, relation, and interpretation.

Why these bodies matter

Semi-corporeal avatars occupy a productive middle range between conventional realism and pure abstraction. They can give up the sealed surface of the mesh body without surrendering bodily legibility. That makes them more than image styles. They become design instruments for altering embodiment, social relation, and the felt perimeter of the self (Eisenstein; Whissel; dos Anjos and Pereira; Glowacki).

The operative question is not whether these bodies look realistic. It is whether they remain coherent when boundaries soften, multiply, or become shareable. When they do, transformable bodies become usable design variables rather than ornamental effects.

Historical and conceptual lineage

Plasmatic form and the refusal of ossification

Sergei Eisenstein remains the clearest historical anchor for thinking about mutable body form. His writing on Disney treats plasmaticness as a refusal of once-and-forever allotted form: a capacity to stretch, melt, and assume other shapes without ceasing to present a living being. Abraham Geil is useful here because he shows how that line is not only about cartoon elasticity. It is also about mimetic form escaping its own fixed identity (Eisenstein; Geil).

Kristen Whissel extends this argument into contemporary cinema. Her account of the digital multitude and the morph shows that collective and unstable forms are not empty spectacle. They carry meaning. Swarms, morphs, and many-part bodies can signal threshold, loss of individuation, release from rigid embodiment, or entry into another order of relation (Whissel).

Ghost matter, animated spirit, and apparition

A second lineage comes from modernism, animation theory, and ghost history. Leigh Wilson offers a vocabulary for ectoplasm, extrusion, and animated copying; Scott Bukatman shows how spirit imagery and animation share a poetics of liveliness; and Susan Owens makes clear that apparition is never just a generic ethereal effect but a historically specific visual grammar (Wilson; Bukatman; Owens).

Taken together, these sources suggest that non-solid embodiment should not be treated as a single mood. Aura, swarm, residue, ghost, and animated spirit each imply different histories and different kinds of relation. That distinction matters once such forms are translated into avatars and interactive systems.

Contemporary XR precedents

Unrealistic bodies can still be inhabited

XR research already shows that embodiment does not depend on a realistic mesh body. Richards and colleagues explored presence in an unrealistic spectral body through proprioceptive illusion. More recently, dos Anjos and Pereira treated abstraction, mesh-based representation, and point-cloud representation as explicit avatar-design variables rather than a simple ladder from low to high realism (Richards et al.; dos Anjos and Pereira).

A related design strand appears in Liedgren, Desmet, and Gaggioli's account of liminal design, which treats transition, framing, and suspension of disbelief as central parts of an experience rather than afterthoughts. Semi-corporeal form often succeeds in exactly that way: through staging as much as depiction (Liedgren et al.).

Boundary-softening can be designed on purpose

Glowacki's Isness work gives the strongest contemporary precedent for diffuse, luminous, collectively merging bodies as intentional experiential design. Participants are not merely shown abstract avatars. They are situated inside a guided environment where soft boundaries, energetic coalescence, and altered self-other distinction are integral to the experience itself (Glowacki et al.; Glowacki).

Desnoyers-Stewart and collaborators add an interaction precedent. Their ETC system shows how abstract aura-like avatars, synchronized audiovisual feedback, and heartbeat-linked representation can make non-solid embodiment socially operative. The avatar here is not just a shell. It is an interaction grammar for relational touch at a distance (Desnoyers-Stewart et al.).

A design synthesis

The literature converges on four design dimensions that make semi-corporeal embodiment legible and useful.

1. Materiality

The body no longer has to appear as a sealed surface. Reeves's particle-systems paper remains the canonical computational reference for fuzzy, dynamic, volumetric form, while Reynolds shows how many locally acting agents can read as one coordinated moving entity (Reeves; Reynolds).

2. Coherence

Loosened form still needs to remain re-identifiable. Wagemans and colleagues provide the perceptual frame for grouping and figure-ground organization, while Johansson shows that a sparse array of moving points can still evoke a compelling human figure. Full surface closure is therefore not the only route to bodily readability (Wagemans et al.; Johansson).

3. Relation

Semi-corporeal bodies become especially interesting when they mediate relation. Glowacki emphasizes coalescence and distributed identity; ETC turns aura-touch into an actionable interaction pattern; and Yee and Bailenson supply the behavioral layer by showing that altered self-representation can feed back into how people act (Glowacki; Desnoyers-Stewart et al.; Yee and Bailenson).

4. Framing

These bodies do not mean the same thing in every setting. Their significance depends on framing: whether the body is read as an aura, an apparition, a collective field, or a transformable cartoon-like organism. Framing also includes pacing and invitation: the experiential scaffolding that makes transformation intelligible instead of arbitrary. That is why the media-theory and cultural-history sources matter alongside the XR work (Wilson; Bukatman; Owens; Whissel; Liedgren et al.).

Design implications

Stable centers, mutable peripheries

A body can become diffuse without becoming unreadable if some anchors remain stable. Recognizable cores, recurring rhythms, or motion signatures can hold coherence while the periphery blurs, splits, trails, or swarms (Wagemans et al.; Johansson).

Permeability should be stateful

Semi-corporeality works best as a changing condition rather than a static look. Bodies can gather, disperse, overlap, merge, and re-individuate. Treating permeability as a state machine rather than a shader choice aligns better with both plasmatic theory and distributed simulation logic (Eisenstein; Reeves; Reynolds).

Merge should be staged, not toggled

Boundary-softening only becomes meaningful when approach, invitation, coalescence, and return are designed as distinct phases. Glowacki's work in particular suggests that the transition into shared or softened embodiment needs context, pacing, and ritual cues if it is to register as an experience rather than an effect (Glowacki et al.; Glowacki; Liedgren et al.).

Symbolic register matters

Ghost, swarm, aura, and plasmatic organism are not interchangeable labels. Each carries a different affective and historical charge. Design choices about non-solid embodiment therefore need to choose their symbolic register deliberately instead of collapsing every weakly bounded body into a generic ethereal style (Owens; Wilson; Whissel).

Plasmatic multitudes are not an argument for vagueness. They are an argument for bodies that stay legible while becoming more permeable, more collective, and more transformable than the default avatar shell usually allows.
